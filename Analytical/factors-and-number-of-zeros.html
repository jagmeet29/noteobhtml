Factors and Number of Zeros

Factors

Factors are numbers that can divide another number exactly, without leaving a remainder. For instance, the factors of 24 are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, and 24.

The core technique for solving all factor-related problems is prime factorization, which involves expressing a number as a product of its prime factors raised to a power.

  • Example (24): 24 = 8 × 3 = 2³ × 3¹
  • Example (2400): 2400 = 24 × 100 = (2³ × 3¹) × (2² × 5²) = 2⁵ × 3¹ × 5²

Types of Factor-Based Questions

Using prime factorization, you can answer seven common types of questions about factors.

1. Total Number of Factors

  • Method: If a number's prime factorization is p₁ᵃ × p₂ᵇ × p₃ᶜ…, the total number of factors is the product of the powers each increased by one: (a+1)(b+1)(c+1)…
  • Example (24 = 2³ × 3¹): (3+1)(1+1) = 4 × 2 = 8 factors
  • Example (2400 = 2⁵ × 3¹ × 5²): (5+1)(1+1)(2+1) = 6 × 2 × 3 = 36 factors

One is added as is 1 for all prime factors.

2. Number of Prime Factors

  • Method: Count the number of unique prime bases in the factorization.
  • Example (24 = 2³ × 3¹): There are two prime factors: 2 and 3.
  • Example (2400 = 2⁵ × 3¹ × 5²): There are three prime factors: 2, 3, and 5.

3. Number of Composite Factors

  • Method: Total Factors - Number of Prime Factors - 1. The number 1 is subtracted because it is neither prime nor composite.
  • Example (24): 8 (total) - 2 (prime) - 1 = 5 composite factors
  • Example (2400): 36 (total) - 3 (prime) - 1 = 32 composite factors

4. Number of Odd Factors

  • Method: Ignore the factor of 2 and apply the "Total Number of Factors" formula to the remaining odd prime factors.
  • Example (24 = 2³ × 3¹): Using only 3¹, the number of odd factors is (1+1) = 2.
  • Example (2400 = 2⁵ × 3¹ × 5²): Using 3¹ × 5², the number of odd factors is (1+1)(2+1) = 6.

5. Number of Even Factors

  • Method 1: Total Factors - Odd Factors.
  • Method 2 (Direct): Take the power of 2 as it is (do not add 1) and multiply by the (power+1) of all other prime factors. For a number 2ᵃ × p₂ᵇ…, the formula is a × (b+1)….
  • Example (24): The number of even factors is 8 - 3 = 5.
  • Example (2400): The number of even factors is 36 - 12 = 24.

6. Number of Factors that are Perfect Squares

  • Method: For a number N = p₁ᵃ × p₂ᵇ × p₃ᶜ…, take the exponents, divide each by 2 and take the floor, and multiply these to determine the total number of factors that are perfect squares.
  • Example (24 = 2³ × 3¹): The total factors that are perfect squares is floor(3/2) floor(1/2) = 10 = 0.
  • Example (36 = 2² × 3²): The total factors that are perfect squares is floor(2/2) floor(2/2) = 11 = 1.

7. Number of Factors that are powers of a single prime

  • Method: For a number N = p₁ᵃ × p₂ᵇ × p₃ᶜ…, take each exponent, subtract one and take the sum.
  • Example (24 = 2³ × 3¹): The total number of factors that are powers of a single prime is (3-1) + (1-1) = 2 + 0 = 2.
  • Example (36 = 2² × 3²): The total number of factors that are powers of a single prime is (2-1) + (2-1) = 1 + 1 = 2.

Factorials

A factorial is the product of all positive integers up to a given number (n!). For example, 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1. The main type of question related to factorials involves finding the number of trailing zeros in its expanded value.

Finding Trailing Zeros

Trailing zeros are created by pairs of 2 and 5 in the prime factorization of a number. Since factors of 2 are more abundant, the number of trailing zeros is determined by the number of factors of 5.

  • Method: To find the number of trailing zeros in n!, continuously divide n by 5 and sum the quotients, ignoring any remainders.
  • Example (56!):
    • 56 ÷ 5 = 11
    • 11 ÷ 5 = 2
    • Total trailing zeros = 11 + 2 = 13
  • Example (154!):
    • 154 ÷ 5 = 30
    • 30 ÷ 5 = 6
    • 6 ÷ 5 = 1
    • Total trailing zeros = 30 + 6 + 1 = 37

Operations with Trailing Zeros

  • Addition (e.g., 100! + 200!): When adding factorials, the number of trailing zeros is determined by the term with the minimum number of zeros. For 100! (24 zeros) and 200! (49 zeros), the sum will have 24 trailing zeros.
  • Multiplication (e.g., 100! × 200!): When multiplying factorials, the number of trailing zeros is the sum of the zeros from each term. The product of 100! and 200! will have 24 + 49 = 73 trailing zeros.
