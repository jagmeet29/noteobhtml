Atom Structure:

  • Nucleus: Protons (+ charge) & Neutrons (no charge)
  • Electrons: (-) charge, orbit nucleus in "shells" at varying distances.
  • Energy: Increases with shell radius (further = higher energy).
  • Valence Electrons: Outermost shell electrons – determine chemical activity.

Semiconductors & Periodic Table:

  • Grouped by Valence Electrons: Periodic table organizes elements by these.
  • Elemental Semiconductors: Silicon (Si) & Germanium (Ge) - Group IV.
  • Compound Semiconductors: Gallium Arsenide (Group III-V).
  • Key Groups: III & V are also important in semiconductors.

Key takeaway: Valence electrons dictate how atoms interact, and the periodic table helps us understand semiconductor properties based on electron arrangement.

Silicon & Semiconductor Basics

  • Silicon Structure: Starts with 5 isolated Si atoms, each with 4 valence electrons.
  • Bonding: When Si atoms get close, valence electrons share to form covalent bonds.
  • Crystal Formation: Results in a tetrahedral structure – each Si atom has 4 nearest neighbors. (Common to Ge, GaAs etc.)
  • Lattice: Forms a repeating lattice structure (see Fig 1.1c).
  • Key Property: Outer edges have available valence electrons – allows for growth of large, single crystals.

Essentially: Silicon forms strong covalent bonds in a tetrahedral arrangement, creating a lattice that can grow into large crystals due to available electrons on the edges.

Silicon & Conductivity

  • Silicon at 0K:

    • 2D representation shows covalent bonds (valence electrons linking atoms).
    • All bonding positions filled - electrons bound to atoms.
    • Therefore, acts as an insulator - no charge flow.

  • Crystal Formation & Energy Bands:

    • Atoms forming crystal = electrons occupy allowed energy bands.
    • 0K: All valence electrons in valence band.
    • Increased Temp: Electrons gain thermal energy.

  • Bandgap & Conduction:

    • To break covalent bond & become free, electron needs min. energy = Eg (bandgap energy).
    • Electrons gaining Eg move to conduction band - become free electrons.
    • Flow of free electrons in conduction band = current.

  • Energy Band Diagram:

    • Ev = max energy of valence band.
    • Ec = min energy of conduction band.
    • Eg = Ec - Ev = bandgap energy.
    • Region between Ev & Ec = forbidden bandgap – electrons can't exist here.
    • Electron moving from valence to conduction band = generation.

  • Material Classification (based on Eg):

    • Insulators: Large Eg (3-6 eV) – few to no free electrons at room temp.
    • Conductors: Many free electrons at room temp.
    • Semiconductors: Eg ~ 1 eV – intermediate conductivity.

Electron Concentration
  • Semiconductors are (charge) neutral overall.
  • Breaking covalent bonds creates free electrons and positive “holes” (empty states).
  • Increased temperature more free electrons & holes.
  • Holes behave like positive charge carriers.
  • Current is carried by both electrons (negative) and holes (positive).
  • Electron & hole concentrations are key parameters affecting current.

Hole Movement:
  • A valence electron can move into an adjacent hole.
  • This effectively causes the hole to move to the location where the electron came from.
  • Hole movement is the collective result of electrons filling vacancies.
  • Although electrons are the actual moving particles, the hole concept simplifies analysis by treating it as a positive charge carrier.

Why Use Resistor Colour Codes?

  • Resistors come in many values.
  • Printing the exact resistance value on small resistors is impractical because the numbers would be tiny and hard to read.
  • To identify resistor values easily, a colour code system was developed.
  • This system uses coloured bands painted on the resistor body to represent resistance and tolerance.
  • The physical size of the resistor often indicates its power rating (wattage).

The Resistor Colour Code System

Colour Bands:

  • Typically, resistors have 4, 5, or 6 coloured bands.
  • Bands are read left to right, starting with the band closest to one lead.
  • The last band (usually wider or separated) indicates tolerance (accuracy).
  • Colours correspond to digits, multipliers, and tolerance values.

How to Read 4-Band Resistors:

  1. First band = 1st digit
  2. Second band = 2nd digit
  3. Third band = multiplier (power of 10)
  4. Fourth band = tolerance

Example:

Tolerance and Precision

  • Tolerance shows how much the actual resistance can vary from the stated value.
  • General-purpose carbon resistors or resistors with no tolerance may have tolerances up to ±20%.
  • Precision resistors may have ±2% with lower tolerance being more expensive
  • Film resistors can have tighter tolerances from ±1% to ±10%
  • Tolerance based on resistance band number:
    • 4 Band: 5%, 10%, 20%
    • 5 Band: 1%, 2%

Surface Mount Device (SMD) Resistors

  • Small rectangular resistors soldered directly onto circuit boards.
  • Marked with 3 or 4 digit numerical codes:
    • First two digits = significant figures
    • Third digit = multiplier (power of 10)

Examples:

  • 103 = 10 × 10³ = 10kΩ
  • 392 = 39 × 10² = 3.9kΩ
  • 105 = 10 × 10⁵ = 1MΩ
  • Values less than 10Ω use R to denote decimal points (e.g., 4R7 = 4.7Ω).
  • "000" markings indicate zero-ohm resistors (basically jumpers).

  • Surface mount resistors use numerical codes for identification.

Resistors: Complete Overview

What is a Resistor?

A resistor is a passive electronic component that resists or limits the flow of electric current in a circuit. It does not generate energy but dissipates it as heat. Its primary function is to regulate current and divide voltages. The electrical resistance is measured in Ohms (Ω).

Symbols and Function

  • Schematic Symbols:
    • Zig-zag line (used mainly in the US)
    • Rectangle (used internationally)
  • Role in Circuits:
    • Voltage divider
    • Current limiter
    • Pull-up or pull-down configuration in digital logic
    • Biasing active components (e.g., transistors)

Types of Resistors

Resistors come in fixed and variable forms. The most common types of fixed resistors fall into four main categories:

1. Carbon Composition Resistors

  • Material: Mixture of carbon dust and ceramic binder.
  • Features:
    • Cheap and widely used
    • High tolerance (±5% to ±20%)
    • Low power (¼ W to 5 W)
    • Low inductance → suitable for high-frequency use
    • Noisy and thermally unstable at higher temperatures
  • Prefix: CR (e.g., CR10kΩ)

2. Film Resistors

Includes Carbon Film, Metal Film, and Metal Oxide Film:

  • Construction:
    • Thin film deposited on ceramic substrate
    • Laser-trimmed into a helical pattern to fine-tune resistance
  • Types:
    • Carbon Film (CF): Lower cost, better than carbon composition
    • Metal Film (MFR): Precision, low noise, better temperature stability
    • Metal Oxide: High surge capacity, higher temp rating
    • Thick Film/Cermet: Surface-mount or network resistors
  • Tolerances:
    • CF: ±5%
    • MFR: ±1%, ±0.5%, ±0.25%, ±0.1%
  • Resistance Range: Up to 10 MΩ

3. Wirewound Resistors

  • Construction: A metal wire (often Nichrome) wound around a ceramic core
  • Features:
    • High precision (as low as 0.01Ω)
    • High power ratings (1W to over 300W)
    • High stability
    • Acts like an inductor → not suitable for high-frequency AC circuits unless non-inductive versions are used
  • Applications:
    • Power supplies
    • Motor controllers
    • Load banks
  • Packages:
    • WH (aluminium clad)
    • W (vitreous enamelled)

4. Semiconductor Resistors

  • Found in ICs and SMD packages
  • Extremely precise and small
  • Used in high-frequency and precision analog circuits

Resistor Characteristics

  • Resistance (Ω): Measures how much the resistor opposes current
  • Tolerance: Deviation from nominal value (e.g., ±5%)
  • Power Rating (W): Maximum power it can dissipate without damage
  • Temperature Coefficient: Change in resistance with temperature
  • Noise: Random fluctuations in voltage
  • Frequency Response: Particularly important in AC circuits

Applications of Resistors

  • Current Limiting: Prevent LEDs from burning out
  • Voltage Division: Two resistors in series create a desired voltage
  • Biasing: Used in transistor circuits
  • Timing: In RC (resistor-capacitor) circuits for filters or oscillators
  • Heat Generation: In heaters and braking resistors

Fixed vs. Variable Resistors

  • Fixed: One resistance value (e.g., 100Ω)
  • Variable: Resistance can be adjusted (e.g., potentiometers or rheostats)

Important Note

All resistors exhibit a positive resistance—they can’t have a negative resistance in conventional usage. They are used in virtually all electrical and electronic devices.

where does the Electric filed comes form ?
Answer

The electric field forms due to charge separation at the PN junction.

Initially, holes diffuse from the P-side to the N-side, and electrons from N to P, uncovering fixed charges (positive ions in N, negative in P). This separation of charge creates an electric field pointing from the positive to negative charge.

Depletion Region & Built-in Potential

  • Diffusion:
    • Holes diffuse from p-region to n-region.
    • Electrons diffuse from n-region to p-region.
  • Charge Separation: Diffusion uncovers ionized acceptor (p-side) and donor (n-side) atoms, creating a charge separation.
  • Space Charge/Depletion Region: Region depleted of mobile carriers due to charge separation.
  • Built-in Potential (): Potential difference across the depletion region.
    • at (thermal voltage).
    • : Acceptor concentration (p-region).
    • : Donor concentration (n-region).
    • : Intrinsic carrier concentration.

Breakdown Voltage

  • Breakdown occurs when reverse bias causes electric field in depletion region to break covalent bonds, creating electron-hole pairs.

  • This leads to a large reverse current, limited by external circuit.

  • Excessive current can cause device burnout.

  • Avalanche Breakdown:

    • Occurs when carriers gain enough kinetic energy from high electric field to break bonds via collisions.
    • Creates an avalanche of electron-hole pairs.
    • Breakdown voltage influenced by doping concentrations – higher doping = lower breakdown voltage.

  • Zener Breakdown:

    • Results from carrier tunneling across the junction.
    • Prominent at very high doping concentrations ().

  • Breakdown voltage typically ranges from 50-200V, but can vary widely (up to >1000V).

  • PIV (Peak Inverse Voltage): The maximum reverse voltage a diode can withstand without breakdown. Must not be exceeded in circuit operation.

  • Zener Diodes: Specifically designed to operate in the breakdown region.
